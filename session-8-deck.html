Passkeys first — there's nothing to phish

  • 1What it is: a cryptographic key pair — the private half stays on your device, the public half lives with the service. No password to type.
  • 2Set one up live: identity provider → Security → Add a passkey → confirm with biometric/PIN → sign out and back in to test.
  • 3Why phishing-resistant: the key only works on the genuine domain — a fake login page gets nothing reusable.
🔑

Password

A shared secret — type it anywhere, and a fake site can catch it.

📱

Passkey

Device-bound — nothing to type, nothing to leak, bound to the real site.

The shift: stop protecting a secret you can be tricked out of — carry a key that can't be handed over.

Hardening, in priority order

🔑

1Passkey

Wherever it's supported — strongest and phishing-resistant. Start here.

📲

2MFA on legacy

For accounts with no passkey yet. Prefer app or hardware over SMS.

🧩

3Unique strong passphrase

One per account. Length + unpredictability beats symbol-soup.

🗄️

4Password manager

Generates and holds the long tail so you never reuse.

Don't skip to #4 and stop. The order is the point — and a Mastery move: audit "connected apps," revoke stale access.

"It's me — I need money now" 📞

Voice clones need only seconds of audio. "It sounded just like them" is no longer proof. Run the script, not the feeling.

📵

1Hang up

Real emergencies survive a callback. The script relies on you not breaking contact.

☎️

2Call back on a known number

The one already in your contacts — never a number the caller gives you.

🔐

3Ask the safe question

A pre-agreed code word a cloned voice or scammer won't know.

Judgment checkpoint: urgency + secrecy + "send now" is the red flag, not the proof. Slow it down; verify before you act or pay.

Phish-test debrief — no names, no blame

Remember the email from a few weeks back? That was us. Clicking is the most common, most human outcome — and the reason we train.

🕵️

The tells

  • Sender domain — close but not quite right
  • Urgency — "act now or lose access"
  • Link mismatch — text says one thing, hover says another
  • Unexpected attachment you didn't ask for
✅

The one check

What single move would have caught it?

  • Hover the link before you click
  • Read the real sender domain, not the display name
  • When unsure: go to the site directly, don't follow the email
No shame, ever. We don't ask who clicked — we ask what check catches it next time.
