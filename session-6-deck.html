What an LLM actually is

It completes likely text. It does not recall facts or look things up — unless a tool is attached. That one truth explains every way it fails.

🎲

Token prediction, not memory

It picks the next likely word, again and again. Sounds like knowing — it's sophisticated autocomplete.

🪄

Hallucination

When the likely-sounding answer is simply wrong. Fluent and specific is not the same as correct.

📏

Context limits

It only "remembers" a window of the chat. In a long conversation it forgets what you said earlier.

📅

Training cutoff

It knows nothing after its training date — and no live access unless a search tool is wired in.

Live demo: ask it something past its cutoff and watch it answer confidently anyway. Confidence is the tell, not the proof.

The 4 Prompt Pillars

  • RRole — who the AI should be. "You are a science tutor for grade-11 students."
  • TTask — the exact job. "Explain the greenhouse effect in 3 short paragraphs."
  • CContext — what it must know. "We've covered photosynthesis, not atmospheric chemistry; no equations."
  • OOutput format — the shape. "Plain language, one analogy, end with two review questions."

❌ Weak prompt

"write about climate change"

Vague in, vague out — the model has to guess everything.

✅ Strong prompt

Role + Task + Context + Output, stacked.

Same model, far better answer — the prompt did the thinking.

The rewrite move: if the output is bad, the prompt was thin. Add the missing pillar — don't just hit regenerate.

Agentic AI — when the intern can press buttons

Some assistants don't just suggest — they act: browse, book, buy, file, send. The moment AI can change the world, you scope it and you gate it.

THE HEART OF THE SESSION ♥

The verification habit — fluency is not evidence

The rule for every AI output: check it against one independent source. We go back to the hook — and catch the planted hallucination together.

🪄

1The planted claim

Confident, specific, wrong. e.g. "the first email was sent in 1965 by Vint Cerf." Smooth — and false.

🔎

2One independent source

Open a reference that doesn't come from the same answer. It disagrees on the date and the person.

✅

3Caught

OPEN → CAUGHT. The source is the proof. Name how you knew — that's the whole skill.

Judgment checkpoint: Delegate low-stakes, easily-checked work · Verify anything you'll submit, share, or act on · Do it yourself when the doing is the learning (or the data is private).

Integrity & disclosure — then sign

✅ Allowed — no disclosure

  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Debugging / explaining a concept

A learning aid — you reproduce the understanding

📝 Allowed — with disclosure

  • Drafting prose you'll submit
  • Summarizing sources (still verify + cite)

The words aren't fully yours — say so

🚫 Never allowed

  • Exams / graded assessments
  • Feedback on a classmate (third-party data)

Defeats the measure · breaks privacy

The privacy rule: never paste personal, family, or third-party private data into a public AI tool — names, addresses, ID numbers, phone numbers, photos, private messages. Sign the policy now on /learn.
THINK LIKE A BUILDER

Delegating to AI is decomposition

Builder's move
With AI
🧩 Break the big task into parts
→
Scope each sub-task before handing it off
🎯 Hand each part to the right tool
→
A 4-Pillar prompt per piece
🐞 Test each part on the way back

Verify each output like debugging
Problem-solving competency: a builder doesn't approve the black box — they decompose it into checkable pieces and verify each one. Same tool, better result.

Assignment 6 — one research task, done twice

The do-it-twice task

  • Pick one small researchable question
  • (1) Manual — your own searching and reading
  • (2) AI-assisted — a 4-Pillar prompt, then verify
  • Submit both outputs + a half-page comparison: where AI saved time, where it was wrong, what you verified + the source
  • Disclose your AI use per the signed policy

Grading bar — AI Literacy (0–3)
